About

Not a pivot.
A through-line.

I’m often asked how I went from civil engineering to Peace Corps to global policy to environmental economics to — now — energy systems. The honest answer: I didn’t change fields. I found better tools for the same questions.

2018
Engineering

I learned to build things — then asked who they were built for.

Santa Clara University. Civil engineering degree from the Bay Area, honors program. The technical training was excellent. But I kept running into questions that equations couldn’t answer: who decides where infrastructure goes? Who gets access to water, to land, to power? I wanted to get closer to those questions, not farther.

2019
Field Work

I went to see how things actually happen on the ground.

Peace Corps, Benin. Working in a small village called Coussi, designing environmental programs for 175+ students, entirely in French. What I really learned wasn’t agriculture — it was the gap between a policy as written and a policy as lived. Community trust. Informal systems. Why interventions that look good on paper disappear the moment an outside organization leaves.

Then COVID hit. Evacuated in March 2020, mid-service. It forced a question I’d been circling: how do I take everything I’ve seen and make it rigorous?

2021
Global Policy

I found the methods to match the questions.

Notre Dame’s Keough School of Global Affairs, Master of Global Affairs in Sustainable Development. Within months I was in Malawi with Catholic Relief Services, leading field research on a national aid program — 28 focus groups, 40 key informant interviews, two contradictory prior evaluations to untangle. I learned that evaluation isn’t just a technical problem. It’s a political one. Who collects what data, why, and what gets done with it are never neutral choices.

2022–
PhD · Yale

Now I’m building the econometric tools to answer the policy questions.

Yale School of the Environment. Causal inference, property rights, land tenure, resource access. I’ve spent the last two years also embedded in clean energy policy — developing curriculum for 120 mid-career professionals, consulting for UNDP on a 30-project M&E strategy, serving on Notre Dame’s advisory council.

The thread connecting all of it: I want to understand whether interventions actually work, for whom, and why — and I want that analysis to drive real decisions. That question applies as much to a carbon credit scheme as to a land titling program in West Africa.

What I’m actually after

Resource access — land, energy, water, capital — and the institutions that determine who gets it. I’m technology-agnostic and sector-agnostic. What I care about is whether something is actually sustainable: financially, politically, socially. Not just whether it sounds good in a proposal.

I move comfortably between technical conversations, cost-effectiveness frameworks, behavioral analysis, and what it takes to get real buy-in — from investors, from communities, from regulators. I’ve done all of these in the same project. That range is the thing I’m most proud of, and it’s what I’m looking to put to work.

I’m actively looking for full-time roles and taking on consulting projects. If you’re working on a hard problem at the intersection of policy, data, and real-world resource decisions — let’s talk.

What I study

The questions I keep
coming back to

My dissertation is about land tenure. But the questions I find most interesting are bigger than any one research project — and they’ve followed me across every domain I’ve worked in.

◈

The theory of measurement

What data do we collect, why do we collect it instead of other data, how do we present it, and what are the implications of those choices? Every evaluation, every carbon credit, every policy KPI embeds assumptions about what matters. I love pulling those apart.

◎

Causation vs. correlation — and why it matters for policy

Impact evaluation, additionality, counterfactuals. These aren’t just academic concepts — they’re the difference between a program that works and one that looks like it works. I’ve applied this in aid, in M&E strategy, and increasingly in thinking about carbon markets.

⨁

The political economy of adoption

A technically sound policy that can’t win buy-in doesn’t matter. I’m interested in what it actually takes for an idea to get traction — monetarily, politically, socially — and how that shapes what we should propose in the first place.

⊕

Surprising externalities and second-best solutions

Good interventions create unexpected consequences. Dismissing imperfect solutions because they’re not optimal often means doing nothing. I think carefully about tradeoffs, backfire effects, and what it means to design policy in a world full of constraints.

Why this connects to energy: Carbon markets, RECs, distributed generation policy — these are all evaluation problems at their core. Does a carbon credit represent real, additional, permanent emissions reduction? Is a REC actually incentivizing new renewable development or just repricing existing generation? These are additionality questions. I’ve spent years building the tools to answer them.

Cases I find interesting

01
Data centers and energy access

The surge in data center demand has materially increased electricity costs in many markets — disproportionately affecting renters and people who can’t install rooftop solar. A classic case of an externality that’s easy to miss if you’re only counting carbon.

02
Land titling in West Africa and the “native/stranger” problem

Formalizing customary land rights sounds straightforwardly good. In practice, it often ignites tensions around who counts as indigenous — reactivating political categories that informal arrangements had quietly neutralized. The intervention solves one problem and creates three others.

03
RECs and the question of additionality

Renewable Energy Certificates may or may not drive new solar and wind development, depending on market structure and timing. Whether they’re a meaningful climate tool or an accounting exercise is an empirical question — and the answer determines whether they deserve a place in a serious decarbonization strategy.

04
The cost of dismissing oil

Rapidly cutting off fossil fuel investment before alternatives are cost-competitive and reliably scaled has distributional consequences that often fall hardest on people with the fewest options. Being serious about energy transition means being serious about sequencing, not just endpoints.

My dissertation

Property rights interventions and development outcomes

I use causal inference methods to study how property rights programs — land titling, tenure formalization — actually affect the communities they target. Who benefits? Under what conditions? Why do similar programs produce wildly different results in different contexts? The work sits at the intersection of development economics, political economy, and evaluation methodology.

Untangling a national aid program with contradictory findings

Two prior evaluations of the same program had reached opposite conclusions. CRS needed to know who was right — and why. I led 8 weeks of qualitative field research (28 focus groups, 40 key informant interviews) across Malawi to assess program outcomes and explain the divergence.
